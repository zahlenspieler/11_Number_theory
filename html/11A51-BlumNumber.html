Blum number


Given a semiprime n=p⁢q, if both p and q are Gaussian primes with no imaginary part, then n is called a Blum number. The first few Blum numbers are 21, 33, 57, 69, 77, 93, 129, 133, 141, 161, 177, 201, 209, 213, 217, 237, 249, 253, 301, 309, 321, 329, 341, 381, 393, 413, 417, 437, 453, 469, 473, 489, 497, etc., listed in A016105 of Sloane’s OEIS.

A semiprime that is a Blum number is also a semiprime among the Gaussian integers and its prime factors also have no imaginary parts. The other real semiprimes are not semiprimes among the Gaussian integers. For example, 177 can only be factored as 3×59 whether Gaussian integers are allowed or not. 159, on the other hand can be factored as either 3×53 or 3⁢(-i)⁢(2+7⁢i)⁢(7+2⁢i).

Large Blum numbers had applications in cryptography prior to advances in integer factorization by means of quadratic sieves.
